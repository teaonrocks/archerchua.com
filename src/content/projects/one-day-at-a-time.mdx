During Mission X by Youth Corps, I took part in an activity called **One Day At A Time**, a financial simulation about the daily trade-offs low-income families face. Coming from a low-income family myself, the content hit very close to home, but the way it was run did not.

Everything was driven through an Excel spreadsheet. People were squinting at cells, double-checking formulas, and fighting with the UI. The emotional impact was strong, but the interaction felt clunky and pulled you out of the experience.

I felt there was a better way to present the same idea. So I contacted the original creator, shared my perspective as both a participant and someone who had lived that reality, and proposed a digital version.

## What ODAT Is

ODAT is a web-based interactive financial simulation game designed to build empathy for the challenges faced by low-income families. Players step into the shoes of a fictional family, then make a series of everyday financial decisions under tight constraints.

Instead of editing spreadsheet cells, the experience is structured around clear scenario text and prompts, discrete choices with visible outcomes, and a guided flow that mirrors the original activity without dragging participants through Excel. Under the hood, it's a real-time web app that quietly manages state and calculations so players can stay with the story instead of the formulas.

The core goal remains the same: help participants feel, not just calculate, how hard it is to get through “one day at a time”.

## Why I Built It This Way

### Replacing Excel to Restore Immersion

The biggest problem I wanted to solve was immersion.

In the original format, participants constantly asked “Which cell do I change?”, facilitators found themselves explaining formulas and sheet navigation, and the emotional weight of each decision had to compete with spreadsheet friction. Moving the activity into a web app let ODAT hide the math in the background, surface only the decisions and their consequences, and free facilitators to focus on discussion instead of technical support. The design is intentionally simple and text-driven so it keeps the spirit of the original while smoothing out the experience.

### Grounded in Lived Experience

Because I grew up in a low-income family, the scenarios did not feel theoretical. They felt familiar. That shaped my design priorities:

- Keep the tone respectful and realistic
- Show trade-offs that low-income families actually face
- Avoid “gamifying” poverty in a way that trivializes it

I worked with the original creator to make sure that even as the format changed, the underlying intent and message stayed intact.

## What I Learned

### 1. UX Can Make or Break an Educational Activity

The content of One Day At A Time was strong; the delivery held it back. ODAT showed me how much UX matters even in non-commercial, educational tools. Small changes—reducing cognitive load, guiding users step by step, and making the flow linear and clear—kept participants focused on empathy and reflection rather than fighting with the mechanics.

### 2. Collaborating with an Existing Creator

Reaching out to the original creator felt risky—I was essentially saying, “I think your idea is great, but the execution could be improved.” It worked because I approached them with respect and appreciation, brought a concrete proposal instead of just criticism, and kept them in the loop as I iterated. That experience taught me how to work with an existing initiative instead of trying to reinvent everything myself.

## What I Would Improve Next

Looking at the current implementation, there are a few things I would like to add or refine: a facilitator view with notes and debrief questions, better mobile support for participants on phones, and optional variant scenarios to reflect different family types and situations. None of these are in the project yet, but they’re clear next steps if ODAT continues to be used.

## Impact

ODAT came from a very personal place. The original activity resonated with my own background, and building this tool was my way of giving something back to the programme that created it.

For my portfolio, ODAT shows that I can take a real-world, non-technical process and redesign it digitally, that I care about the user’s emotional experience as much as the logic, and that I know how to collaborate with existing stakeholders while respecting the original intent.

Most importantly, it turned something that once described my life into something I could actively improve for others.
